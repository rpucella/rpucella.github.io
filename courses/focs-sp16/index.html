Foundations of Computer Science (Spring 2016)

ENGR 3520 / Thu 16:00-18:30 / AC 318

This course explores the central object of study of Computer Science, namely the notion of computation. We're going to develop formal tools for defining what we mean by computation, through various forms of automata. We'll develop ways to talk about the efficiency of computations, by quantifying their time complexity. We will examine alternative models of computations, and see how they correspond to different programming paradigms: functional programming, logic programming, and object-oriented programming. We will end with a discussion of basic data structures and algorithms.

  • Feb 4:Finite automata

    Some summary lecture notes.

    Read Chapter 3 of the book (The Simplest Computers). Note that the author introduces, after nondeterministic and deterministic finite automata, a slightly more general form, a nondeterministic finite automaton with free moves. As he points out, that still does not let you accept more languages. It just makes it more convenient to manipulate finite automata.

    Notes from Monday's Ninjas section that describe how to construct a regular expression whose language is that accepted by a given finite automaton, showing that finite automata can only accept regular languages.

  • Feb 18:Turing machines

    Notes from the lecture. I mentioned multitape Turing machines in class. These notes go into some detail about them.

    I mentionned at the beginning of class examples of languages that were not regular, such as {anbn | n ≥ 0}, are not regular. We don't have any way right now to show that a language is not regular. The most common tool used is the Pumping Lemma for Regular Languages. Here's a reasonable reference.

    Read Chapter 5 of the Understanding Computation book.

    The Church-Turing thesis.

    If you're curious about what other machines aside from finite automata and Turing machines are used in the field, read through Chapter 4 of the Understanding Computation book, which describes pushdown automata — basically a finite automaton equipped with a stack. This gives you a class of machines that lands midway between finite automata and Turing machines in terms of the class of languages they can accept.

    Some notes from Monday's Ninjas section that describe nondeterministic Turing machines.

  • Apr 7 :Algorithms and complexity

    Determining the running time of recursive functions (i.e., anything you write in Ocaml) requires solving recurrence equations. There is no general method for solving recurrence equations, but many different techniques that apply in subsets of cases. This is a reasonable overview. This goes into more detail about the so-called iteration method: substitute until you spot a pattern.

    The code for arithmetic expressions we developed in class.

    Wikipedia page on binary search trees and on the version of self-balanced binary search trees I described in class, AVL trees. The description of the rotations that restore the balanced height property after an insertion is reasonably clear there. There are basically four cases to consider, depending on the local form of unbalance.

    A cute visualization of what happens when you insert into an AVL tree. Try starting with the empty tree, and inserting 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8... You'll see the tree grow "to the right" and then rebalance itself repeatedly.

    An analysis of the height of AVL trees, showing that it is always logarithmic in the number of nodes.
